WHAT IS THE PROBLEM

The knight's "L-shaped" move is intuitive for humans but ambiguous for a computer. Our goal is to translate this physical rule into a formal, discrete structure that allows for powerful algorithmic analysis.

KNIGHT MOVEMENT

A knight's move can be defined mathematically as a change of $(\Delta r, \Delta c)$ where $\{|\Delta r|, |\Delta c|\} = \{1, 2\}$. This gives us 8 possible move vectors from any given square.

GRAPH PROPERTIES

  • Represent chessboard as graph (64 nodes)
  • Each knight move = edge between nodes
  • Undirected & Unweighted: moves are symmetric, all edges cost the same
  • Degree distribution: corners have 2 moves, edges more, center the most
  • Connected: every square is reachable from any other
  • Symmetric: graph is rotationally and reflectively symmetric
  • Bipartite: knight alternates between black and white squares

HEAT MAP

By running our algorithm on all 64 squares and counting the legal moves (the degree of each node), we can create a "Heatmap of Control."

PATHFINDER

Using our graph model, we can apply a Breadth-First Search (BFS) algorithm to find the mathematically shortest path between any two squares.

WHAT ABOUT THE DANGER

A simple pathfinder is "blind." To solve this, we evolve our model to a weighted graph, where moving to a threatened square has a high "cost."
